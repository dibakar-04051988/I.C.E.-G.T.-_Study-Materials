Q2: Draw and explain the P-V and T-S diagrams for an ideal Otto cycle.

The ideal Otto cycle consists of four processes:

  • 1-2: Isentropic Compression: Air is compressed reversibly and adiabatically. Pressure and temperature increase, volume decreases.
  • 2-3: Constant Volume Heat Addition: Heat is added at constant volume (simulating combustion). Pressure and temperature increase significantly.
  • 3-4: Isentropic Expansion: Hot gases expand reversibly and adiabatically, doing work. Pressure and temperature decrease, volume increases.
  • 4-1: Constant Volume Heat Rejection: Heat is rejected at constant volume (simulating exhaust). Pressure and temperature decrease.

      |     3
      |    / \
      |   /   \
      |  2     4
      | /       \
      |/_________\
      1------------> V
  • 1-2: Curve upwards, V decreases
  • 2-3: Vertical line upwards
  • 3-4: Curve downwards, V increases
  • 4-1: Vertical line downwards

T-S Diagram:

      |     3
      |    /|
      |   / |
      |  2  |

      |/    |4
      1-----/

  • 2-3: Curve upwards and to the right (heat addition)
  • 3-4: Vertical line downwards (S=constant)
  • 4-1: Curve downwards and to the left (heat rejection)

Q3: Explain the significance of Octane Number and Cetane Number for engine fuels.

Octane Number (ON): This rating is applicable to Spark Ignition (SI) engine fuels (like petrol/gasoline). It indicates the fuel's resistance to auto-ignition or knocking (detonation) under high temperature and pressure in the engine cylinder. A higher octane number means the fuel can withstand more compression before igniting spontaneously. Fuels with high ON are required for high-compression SI engines to prevent knocking, which can damage the engine. It is measured by comparing the fuel's knocking characteristics to a mixture of iso-octane (ON=100, high resistance) and n-heptane (ON=0, low resistance).

Cetane Number (CN): This rating is applicable to Compression Ignition (CI) engine fuels (like diesel). It indicates the fuel's ignition quality, specifically the ignition delay period – the time lag between fuel injection and the start of combustion. A higher cetane number means a shorter ignition delay, leading to smoother combustion and easier starting, especially in cold conditions. Fuels with low CN cause a longer ignition delay, leading to more fuel accumulating before ignition, resulting in a rapid pressure rise and diesel knock. It is measured by comparing the fuel's ignition delay to a mixture of cetane (n-hexadecane, CN=100, short delay) and alpha-methylnaphthalene (CN=0, long delay).

A valve timing diagram is a graphical representation showing the precise moments in the engine cycle (in terms of crankshaft angle) at which the inlet and exhaust valves open and close. It also shows the timing of spark ignition. These timings are crucial for efficient engine operation.

Key Events:

  • IVO (Inlet Valve Opens): Typically 10-20° Before Top Dead Centre (BTDC) to allow early entry of charge.
  • IVC (Inlet Valve Closes): Typically 30-50° After Bottom Dead Centre (ABDC) to utilize the inertia of the incoming charge (ram effect).
  • Spark: Occurs typically 5-30° BTDC on the compression stroke to allow time for combustion to develop peak pressure shortly after TDC.
  • EVO (Exhaust Valve Opens): Typically 30-50° Before Bottom Dead Centre (BBDC) during the power stroke to allow exhaust gases to escape due to their own pressure (blowdown), reducing work done during the exhaust stroke.
  • EVC (Exhaust Valve Closes): Typically 5-15° After Top Dead Centre (ATDC) to ensure complete scavenging and sometimes to allow overlap.

Valve Overlap: The period when both inlet and exhaust valves are open simultaneously (around TDC at the end of exhaust stroke and beginning of suction stroke). It helps in better scavenging.

Q5: Classify IC engines based on at least four different criteria.

IC engines can be classified based on various criteria:

  1. Type of Ignition:
    • Spark Ignition (SI) Engine: Combustion is initiated by an electric spark (e.g., petrol engines).
    • Compression Ignition (CI) Engine: Combustion is initiated by the high temperature of compressed air (e.g., diesel engines).
  2. Number of Strokes per Cycle:
    • Four-Stroke Engine: Completes its cycle in four piston strokes (two crankshaft revolutions).
    • Two-Stroke Engine: Completes its cycle in two piston strokes (one crankshaft revolution).
  3. Thermodynamic Cycle:
    • Otto Cycle Engine (Constant Volume Heat Addition): Typically SI engines.
    • Diesel Cycle Engine (Constant Pressure Heat Addition): Early slow-speed CI engines.
    • Dual Combustion Cycle Engine (Part Constant Volume, Part Constant Pressure Heat Addition): Modern high-speed CI engines.
  4. Fuel Used:
    • Petrol Engines: Use gasoline/petrol.
    • Diesel Engines: Use diesel.
    • Gas Engines: Use gaseous fuels like CNG, LPG, Biogas.
    • Multi-fuel Engines: Can operate on more than one type of fuel.
  5. (Other criteria include: Method of cooling (air/water), Cylinder arrangement (in-line, V-type, radial), Application (automotive, marine, stationary)).

Q1: Explain the working principle of a simple carburetor with a neat sketch.

A simple carburetor's primary function is to atomize and vaporize fuel and mix it with air in the correct proportion for engine combustion.

Working Principle: It works on Bernoulli's principle.

  • Float Chamber: Maintains a constant level of fuel. Fuel enters from the fuel tank via a needle valve controlled by a float.
  • Venturi: A restriction in the air passage (choke tube). As air flows from the atmosphere through the air filter and into the venturi, its velocity increases, and according to Bernoulli's principle, its pressure decreases below atmospheric pressure.
  • Fuel Nozzle/Jet: Located at the venturi throat, connected to the float chamber. The pressure difference between the float chamber (atmospheric) and the venturi throat (sub-atmospheric) causes fuel to be drawn out of the nozzle.
  • Atomization & Mixing: The fuel is atomized into fine droplets by the high-velocity air stream and mixes with the air to form a combustible mixture.
  • Throttle Valve: Located after the venturi, it controls the quantity of air-fuel mixture supplied to the engine, thereby controlling engine speed and power.

Sketch:

Air Intake --> Filter -->
                     _________________
                    |                 | --> To Engine
Fuel In --> Float--Needle Valve   |    Cylinder
(from tank) |  Chamber        |  Throttle
            |   (Fuel Level)  |    Valve
            |_________________|     /
                 |    ^ Venturi  /
                 |____|____(Throat)
                      ^ Fuel Nozzle

A simple carburetor has limitations: it tends to provide a lean mixture at low speeds and a rich mixture at high speeds, opposite to engine requirements. It also cannot compensate for altitude changes.

Q2: What are the different types of fuel injection systems used in IC engines? Briefly describe them.

Fuel injection systems deliver precisely metered fuel directly into the intake manifold, port, or cylinder.

Classification:

  1. Based on Location of Injection:
    • Indirect Injection (IDI):
      • Manifold Injection: Fuel is injected into the intake manifold (common in older SI engines, also called Throttle Body Injection - TBI if single point).
      • Port Injection (PFI/MPI): Fuel is injected into the intake port of each cylinder, just before the intake valve (common in modern SI engines).
      • Pre-combustion Chamber/Swirl Chamber Injection: Used in some CI engines (IDI diesels). Fuel is injected into a small auxiliary chamber connected to the main combustion chamber.
    • Direct Injection (DI):
      • Gasoline Direct Injection (GDI): Fuel is injected directly into the combustion chamber in SI engines.
      • Diesel Direct Injection (DI): Fuel is injected directly into the main combustion chamber in CI engines (most common in modern diesels).
  2. Based on Metering and Timing Control:
    • Mechanical Injection Systems: Fuel quantity and timing are controlled by mechanical means (e.g., jerk pumps, distributor pumps in older CI engines).
    • Electronic Fuel Injection (EFI) Systems: Fuel quantity and timing are controlled electronically by an Engine Control Unit (ECU) based on various sensor inputs. This is the standard in modern engines.
  3. Based on Number of Injectors:
    • Single Point Injection (SPI) / Throttle Body Injection (TBI): One or two injectors supply fuel for all cylinders at a central point (usually above the throttle body).
    • Multi-Point Fuel Injection (MPFI/MPI): A separate injector for each cylinder, usually in the intake port.
  4. For CI Engines Specifically:
    • Common Rail Direct Injection (CRDI): High-pressure fuel is stored in a common rail and supplied to electronically controlled injectors. Offers precise control over injection pressure, timing, and multiple injections.
    • Unit Injector System (UIS): Each cylinder has its own integrated pump and injector unit, cam-actuated.
    • Unit Pump System (UPS) / Pump-Line-Nozzle (PLN): Each cylinder has its own pump, but fuel is delivered to the injector via a short high-pressure line.

Q3: Explain the phenomenon of detonation in SI engines. What are its causes and effects?

Detonation (Knocking) in SI Engines:

Detonation is an abnormal combustion phenomenon in SI engines. After the spark plug ignites the air-fuel mixture, a normal flame front travels across the combustion chamber. The unburnt mixture ahead of this flame front (called the "end gas") is compressed and heated by the advancing flame and piston compression. If the temperature and pressure of this end gas reach critical levels, it can auto-ignite spontaneously and rapidly before the flame front arrives. This rapid, uncontrolled combustion of the end gas creates multiple, localized flame fronts that collide, producing high-frequency pressure waves. These waves cause a characteristic sharp metallic "pinging" or "knocking" sound.

Causes of Detonation:

  • Fuel Quality: Low octane number fuel.
  • Engine Design: High compression ratio, combustion chamber shape promoting hot spots.
  • Operating Conditions:
    • Advanced ignition timing.
    • High engine load (leading to higher cylinder pressures and temperatures).
    • Lean air-fuel mixtures (can burn hotter under certain conditions).
    • Overheating of the engine.
    • Carbon deposits in the combustion chamber (act as hot spots and increase compression).

Effects of Detonation:

  • Noise and Vibration: Characteristic knocking sound.
  • Reduced Power and Efficiency: Energy is released less effectively.
  • Increased Heat Transfer: Localized high temperatures can increase heat loss to cylinder walls.
  • Engine Damage:
    • Overheating of spark plug, piston crown, cylinder head.
    • Pitting of piston crown and cylinder head.
    • Can lead to piston ring failure, head gasket failure, or even catastrophic engine failure if severe and prolonged.

Q4: Describe the stages of combustion in a CI engine with a neat P-θ (Pressure-Crank Angle) diagram.

Combustion in a CI engine is heterogeneous and occurs in four distinct stages after fuel is injected into the hot compressed air:

P-θ (Pressure-Crank Angle) Diagram for CI Engine Combustion:

    P ^
      |         C (Max Pressure)
      |        / \
      |       /   \  (Controlled Combustion)
      |      B-----D (Rapid/Uncontrolled Combustion)
      |     /.
      |    / .
      |   /  . (Ignition Delay)
      |  A   .
      | /    .
      |/_____.E_________________> θ (Crank Angle)
    TDC  (Start of Injection)
  • A: Start of fuel injection (a few degrees BTDC).
  • B: Point of ignition (start of combustion).
  • A-B: Ignition Delay Period:
    • This is the period between the start of injection (A) and the start of actual combustion (B).
    • Fuel is injected, atomized, vaporized, mixed with hot air, and undergoes pre-flame chemical reactions.
    • Duration depends on fuel cetane number, air temperature & pressure, droplet size, air turbulence.
  • B-C: Period of Rapid or Uncontrolled Combustion:
    • Fuel accumulated during the delay period ignites rapidly.
    • Sharp rise in pressure and temperature.
    • If the delay period is long, more fuel accumulates, leading to a very rapid pressure rise (diesel knock).
  • C-D: Period of Controlled Combustion:
    • Once the fuel from the delay period has burned, the rate of combustion is controlled by the rate of fuel injection and mixing.
    • Fuel injected during this stage burns as it enters and mixes.
    • Pressure may remain relatively constant (ideal Diesel cycle) or continue to rise and then fall.
  • D-E: Period of Afterburning / Late Combustion:
    • Combustion may continue into the expansion stroke.
    • Caused by incomplete combustion of fuel particles, especially those that were poorly atomized or in fuel-rich zones.
    • This phase is undesirable as it leads to lower efficiency and higher exhaust temperatures.

Q5: What are the air-fuel mixture requirements for an SI engine under different operating conditions (e.g., starting, idling, cruising, acceleration)?

The air-fuel ratio (A/F) requirements for an SI engine vary depending on the operating conditions to ensure smooth operation, power, and efficiency. The stoichiometric A/F ratio (chemically correct for complete combustion) for gasoline is approx. 14.7:1 by mass.

  • Starting (Cold Engine): Very Rich Mixture (A/F ~ 1:1 to 9:1)
    • Reason: Low engine temperature leads to poor fuel vaporization and condensation on cold manifold/cylinder walls. A very rich mixture ensures enough vaporized fuel is present to form a combustible mixture near the spark plug.
  • Idling: Rich Mixture (A/F ~ 10:1 to 12:1)
    • Reason: At low engine speeds and closed throttle, there's significant exhaust gas dilution due to valve overlap and poor scavenging. A rich mixture is needed for smooth and stable idling.
  • Cruising (Normal/Part Load): Lean Mixture (A/F ~ 15:1 to 17:1)
    • Reason: For maximum fuel economy. The engine is warm, and combustion is efficient. A slightly lean mixture ensures complete combustion of fuel.
  • Maximum Power / Full Load Acceleration: Rich Mixture (A/F ~ 12:1 to 13:1)
    • Reason: To obtain maximum power output. A rich mixture burns faster and also helps in cooling the combustion chamber and exhaust valves due to the latent heat of vaporization of excess fuel, preventing overheating and detonation.
  • Deceleration: Lean Mixture or Fuel Cut-off
    • Reason: To reduce emissions and save fuel. Modern engines often cut off fuel supply completely during deceleration.

Problem 1: A 4-cylinder, 4-stroke SI engine has a bore of 80 mm and a stroke of 100 mm. The engine speed is 3000 rpm. If the volumetric efficiency is 80% and the air-fuel ratio is 15:1, calculate the mass flow rate of fuel required. Assume density of air at inlet conditions is 1.18 kg/m³.

Given: No. of cylinders (ncyl) = 4, Bore (D) = 80 mm = 0.08 m, Stroke (L) = 100 mm = 0.1 m, Speed (N) = 3000 rpm, Volumetric efficiency (ηvol) = 80% = 0.8, Air-Fuel Ratio (A/F) = 15:1, Density of air (ρair) = 1.18 kg/m³.

  • Swept Volume per cylinder (Vs_cyl):
    Vs_cyl = (π/4) * D² * L = (π/4) * (0.08)² * 0.1 = 0.00050265 m³
  • Total Swept Volume (Vs_total):
    Vs_total = Vs_cyl * ncyl = 0.00050265 * 4 = 0.0020106 m³
  • Volume flow rate of air (Qair_actual):
    Qair_actual = Vs_total * (N/2) * ηvol (N/2 because it's a 4-stroke engine)
    Qair_actual = 0.0020106 m³ * (3000 / 2) min-1 * 0.8
    Qair_actual = 0.0020106 * 1500 * 0.8 = 2.41272 m³/min
    Qair_actual = 2.41272 / 60 m³/s = 0.040212 m³/s
  • Mass flow rate of air (ṁair):
    ṁair = ρair * Qair_actual = 1.18 kg/m³ * 0.040212 m³/s
    ṁair = 0.04745 kg/s
  • Mass flow rate of fuel (ṁfuel):
    A/F = ṁair / ṁfuel
    ṁfuel = ṁair / (A/F) = 0.04745 kg/s / 15
    ṁfuel = 0.003163 kg/s (or 3.163 g/s)

Q1: Define Indicated Power (IP), Brake Power (BP), and Frictional Power (FP). How are they related, and what is mechanical efficiency?

Indicated Power (IP): This is the total power developed by the combustion of fuel inside the engine cylinder. It is the power exerted by the gases on the piston. It can be calculated from the indicator diagram (P-V diagram) of the engine.
IP = (Pim * L * A * nk * Ncyl) / (60 * x)
where Pim = indicated mean effective pressure, L = stroke length, A = piston area, nk = number of power strokes per minute per cylinder (N/2 for 4-stroke, N for 2-stroke), Ncyl = number of cylinders, x=1 for kW.

Brake Power (BP): This is the actual power available at the engine crankshaft (output shaft) that can be used to do useful work. It is always less than IP due to frictional losses. It is measured using a dynamometer.
BP = (2πNT) / 60
where N = engine speed in rpm, T = brake torque in N-m.

Frictional Power (FP): This is the power lost in overcoming friction between moving parts of the engine (piston, bearings, etc.), and also power required to drive engine auxiliaries (like oil pump, water pump, alternator if considered part of engine internal losses).

Relationship: IP = BP + FP

Mechanical Efficiency (ηmech): It is the ratio of brake power to indicated power. It represents how much of the power developed in the cylinder is actually available at the crankshaft.
ηmech = BP / IP
A higher mechanical efficiency indicates lower frictional losses.

Q2: Explain what a Heat Balance Sheet is for an IC engine. What are its major components?

A Heat Balance Sheet is an account of the heat supplied to the engine and its distribution or utilization. It helps in understanding the energy flow and losses within the engine, providing insights into its thermal performance. The total heat supplied by the fuel is balanced against the heat converted into useful work and various heat losses.

Major Components (on a rate basis, e.g., kJ/min or kW):

  1. Heat Supplied (Qs):
    • This is the total heat energy released by the combustion of fuel.
    • Qs = mf * CV (where mf is mass flow rate of fuel, CV is calorific value of fuel). This is taken as 100%.
  2. Heat Distribution/Utilization:
    • a) Heat Equivalent of Brake Power (QBP): This is the useful work output of the engine.
      QBP = BP (in kW) * 60 (if kJ/min)
    • b) Heat Lost to Cooling Medium (Qcooling): Heat carried away by the cooling water (for water-cooled engines) or cooling air (for air-cooled engines).
      Qcooling = mw * cpw * (Two - Twi) for water cooling (mw=mass flow of water, cpw=specific heat, Two, Twi=outlet/inlet temps).
    • c) Heat Lost to Exhaust Gases (Qexhaust): Sensible heat carried away by the hot exhaust gases.
      Qexhaust = mg * cpg * (Tg - Tatm) (mg=mass flow of exhaust, cpg=specific heat, Tg=exhaust temp, Tatm=ambient temp).
    • d) Unaccounted Losses (Qunaccounted): This includes heat lost due to radiation, convection from engine surfaces, incomplete combustion, and measurement errors. It is calculated by difference:
      Qunaccounted = Qs - (QBP + Qcooling + Qexhaust)

The heat balance sheet is typically presented as a table showing each component in energy units (e.g., kJ/min) and as a percentage of the total heat supplied.

Q3: Discuss the working principle and advantages of a Common Rail Direct Injection (CRDI) system.

Working Principle of CRDI:

The Common Rail Direct Injection (CRDI) system is an advanced fuel injection system primarily used in modern diesel (CI) engines, though a similar concept (GDI) is used in petrol engines.

  1. High-Pressure Pump: A high-pressure pump (driven by the engine) draws fuel from the tank and pressurizes it to very high levels (typically 1000 to 2500 bar or even higher).
  2. Common Rail: This pressurized fuel is supplied to a "common rail," which acts as a high-pressure accumulator or reservoir, maintaining a constant high pressure regardless of engine speed or load.
  3. Injectors: Solenoid or piezo-electronically controlled injectors are connected to the common rail. Each injector delivers fuel directly into its respective engine cylinder.
  4. ECU Control: The Engine Control Unit (ECU) precisely controls the timing, duration, and often the number of injections per cycle for each injector. It uses inputs from various sensors (engine speed, load, temperature, pedal position, etc.).

Advantages of CRDI:

  • Independent Control of Injection Pressure: Injection pressure is largely independent of engine speed, allowing high injection pressures even at low speeds, leading to better atomization and combustion.
  • Precise Control of Injection Timing and Quantity: The ECU allows for very accurate control over when and how much fuel is injected.
  • Multiple Injections: CRDI systems can perform multiple injections per combustion cycle (e.g., pilot injection, main injection, post-injection).
    • Pilot injection: Small amount of fuel injected before the main injection to initiate smooth combustion, reducing noise (diesel knock) and NOx.
    • Post-injection: Small amount of fuel injected after the main injection to burn soot particles in the exhaust system (for DPF regeneration) or reduce NOx.
  • Improved Fuel Efficiency: Better atomization and combustion control lead to more complete burning and higher efficiency.
  • Reduced Emissions: More complete combustion and precise control help in reducing harmful emissions like particulate matter (PM) and NOx.
  • Higher Power Output and Torque: Improved combustion efficiency leads to better engine performance.
  • Quieter Operation: Pilot injection significantly reduces diesel knock, leading to smoother and quieter engine operation.

Q4: What is Variable Valve Timing (VVT)? Explain its purpose and briefly how it can be achieved.

Variable Valve Timing (VVT):

VVT is an automotive technology that allows the timing of the engine's intake and/or exhaust valves to be changed (advanced or retarded) while the engine is in operation. In some advanced systems, valve lift and duration can also be varied.

Purpose of VVT:

The primary purpose of VVT is to optimize engine performance across a wide range of operating speeds and loads. Fixed valve timing is always a compromise.

  • Improved Volumetric Efficiency: By adjusting when valves open and close relative to piston position, VVT can improve the engine's ability to draw in air-fuel mixture and expel exhaust gases.
  • Increased Torque and Power: Optimizing valve timing for different RPM ranges broadens the torque curve and increases peak power. E.g., advancing intake timing at low RPM can improve low-end torque, while retarding it at high RPM can improve high-end power.
  • Better Fuel Economy: By improving combustion efficiency and reducing pumping losses (e.g., by using valve overlap to create an internal EGR effect), VVT can reduce fuel consumption.
  • Reduced Emissions: Optimized combustion and internal EGR (Exhaust Gas Recirculation) effect (by increasing valve overlap at part load) can help reduce NOx emissions.
  • Smoother Idling: Specific valve timing can stabilize engine operation at idle.

How VVT can be Achieved (Briefly):

Several mechanisms are used:

  1. Cam Phasing: The most common method. The angular position of the camshaft relative to the crankshaft is altered. This is typically done using a hydraulic actuator (often oil pressure controlled by a solenoid valve directed by the ECU) on the camshaft sprocket or gear. This changes the timing of all valves on that camshaft simultaneously.
  2. Cam Switching/Variable Lift: Systems use multiple cam lobes for each valve (e.g., one for low RPM, one for high RPM). The rocker arm or follower is switched between these lobes at a certain engine speed. (e.g., Honda's VTEC). This can change both timing and lift/duration.
  3. Eccentric Camshaft Drive: The camshaft drive can be made eccentric, allowing phase changes.
  4. Electromechanical/Hydraulic Systems: More advanced systems use fully flexible valve actuation, eliminating the camshaft altogether (camless engines), allowing independent control of timing, lift, and duration for each valve. These are complex and less common in mass production.

Q5: List and briefly explain three methods of improving engine performance.

Improving engine performance generally refers to increasing power output, torque, fuel efficiency, or reducing emissions. Here are three common methods:

  1. Forced Induction (Supercharging/Turbocharging):
    • Explanation: This involves using a compressor to force more air (and thus more oxygen) into the engine cylinders than would be possible with natural aspiration. More air allows more fuel to be burned, resulting in a greater power output from a given displacement.
      • Turbocharging: Uses a turbine driven by the engine's exhaust gases to power the compressor.
      • Supercharging: Uses a compressor driven directly by the engine's crankshaft (via a belt).
    • Effect: Significantly increases power and torque, can improve efficiency if engine is downsized.
  2. Improving Volumetric Efficiency:
    • Explanation: Volumetric efficiency is the ratio of the actual mass of air drawn into the cylinder to the theoretical mass of air that could fill the swept volume at atmospheric conditions. Improving it means getting more air-fuel mixture into the cylinders.
    • Methods:
      • Optimized Intake and Exhaust Manifold Design: Smoother, tuned-length runners.
      • Variable Valve Timing (VVT) and Lift: Adjusts valve operation for optimal airflow at different engine speeds.
      • Multiple Valves per Cylinder: (e.g., 4 valves) provide greater port area for airflow.
      • Port Polishing: Smoothing intake and exhaust ports to reduce flow resistance.
    • Effect: Increases power and torque, can improve fuel efficiency.
  3. Increasing Compression Ratio:
    • Explanation: The compression ratio is the ratio of the volume in the cylinder when the piston is at BDC to the volume when it's at TDC. Increasing the compression ratio generally increases the thermal efficiency of the engine (as seen in Otto and Diesel cycle efficiency formulas). A higher compression ratio means the air-fuel mixture is compressed more, leading to higher pressure and temperature at the end of compression, resulting in more work output during expansion.
    • Limitations: For SI engines, it's limited by the fuel's octane rating to prevent knocking. For CI engines, higher compression ratios improve cold starting and efficiency but can lead to higher NOx.
    • Effect: Increases thermal efficiency (better fuel economy) and power output.

Module 5: Alternate Fuels For IC Engines

Short Answer Type Questions (5 marks each):

Q1: Discuss the need for alternate fuels for IC engines.

The strong reliance on conventional fossil fuels (petrol and diesel) for IC engines has led to several pressing issues, necessitating the search for and adoption of alternate fuels. The key needs are:

  1. Energy Security: Many countries are heavily dependent on importing crude oil, making their economies vulnerable to geopolitical instabilities, price volatility, and supply disruptions. Developing indigenous alternate fuels can enhance national energy security.
  2. Environmental Concerns: Combustion of fossil fuels releases greenhouse gases (GHGs) like CO2, contributing to global warming and climate change. They also produce pollutants like NOx, SOx, particulate matter (PM), and unburnt hydrocarbons, which cause air pollution and health problems. Some alternate fuels offer potential for lower GHG emissions (e.g., biofuels if sustainably sourced) and reduced harmful pollutants.
  3. Depletion of Fossil Fuel Reserves: Crude oil is a finite resource. As global demand increases, reserves are depleting, leading to eventual scarcity and higher extraction costs. Alternate fuels, especially renewable ones, offer a sustainable long-term solution.
  4. Economic Benefits: Developing a domestic alternate fuel industry can create jobs, stimulate rural economies (e.g., through biofuel production), and reduce expenditure on oil imports.
  5. Stricter Emission Regulations: Governments worldwide are implementing increasingly stringent emission standards for vehicles. Alternate fuels can help meet these targets, sometimes more easily than with conventional fuels.

Q2: What are alcohol fuels? Mention two common alcohol fuels and list their advantages and disadvantages for use in IC engines.

Alcohol fuels are organic compounds containing one or more hydroxyl (-OH) groups. They can be produced from various sources, including biomass (bio-alcohols) or fossil fuels.

Two common alcohol fuels are:

  1. Methanol (CH3OH)
  2. Ethanol (C2H5OH)

Advantages of Alcohol Fuels (e.g., Ethanol):

  • High Octane Number: Alcohols generally have higher octane numbers than gasoline, allowing for higher compression ratios in SI engines, potentially leading to higher thermal efficiency and power.
  • Reduced Emissions: They tend to produce lower carbon monoxide (CO) and unburnt hydrocarbon (HC) emissions. Ethanol is an oxygenated fuel, which can promote more complete combustion.
  • Renewable Source (for bio-alcohols): Ethanol can be produced from fermentation of sugars or starches (from crops like corn, sugarcane) or cellulose. Methanol can be made from biomass, natural gas, or coal. Bio-alcohols can reduce reliance on fossil fuels.
  • Wider Flammability Limits: Can operate with leaner mixtures than gasoline.
  • High Latent Heat of Vaporization: This leads to a cooling effect on the intake charge, increasing its density (volumetric efficiency) and reducing knocking tendency.

Disadvantages of Alcohol Fuels:

  • Lower Energy Density: Alcohols have lower energy content per unit volume or mass compared to gasoline. This means more fuel is needed to travel the same distance (lower km/L).
  • Corrosiveness: Alcohols can be corrosive to certain metals (like aluminum, magnesium) and elastomers (plastics, rubber) commonly found in fuel systems. Engine modifications might be needed.
  • Poor Cold Starting: Due to their high latent heat of vaporization and low vapor pressure, pure alcohols can cause difficulty in starting engines in cold weather. Gasoline blending or starting aids are often required.
  • Hygroscopic Nature (Ethanol): Ethanol readily absorbs water from the atmosphere, which can lead to phase separation if blended with gasoline, and potential corrosion issues.
  • Material Compatibility Issues: Requires modifications to fuel tanks, lines, and injector components.
  • Production Challenges: Large-scale production of bio-alcohols can compete with food production for land and resources (food vs. fuel debate).

Q3: What is Biodiesel? How is it produced (briefly) and what are its key properties relevant to CI engines?

Biodiesel is a renewable, biodegradable fuel manufactured domestically from vegetable oils (e.g., soybean, rapeseed, palm, jatropha), animal fats, or recycled cooking grease. Chemically, it consists of mono-alkyl esters of long-chain fatty acids. It is typically used in CI (diesel) engines, either in pure form (B100) or blended with petroleum diesel (e.g., B20 is 20% biodiesel, 80% petroleum diesel).

Production (Briefly - Transesterification):

The most common method for producing biodiesel is transesterification.

  1. Reactants: Vegetable oil or animal fat (which are triglycerides) are reacted with an alcohol (commonly methanol, sometimes ethanol) in the presence of a catalyst (typically sodium hydroxide - NaOH, or potassium hydroxide - KOH).
  2. Process: The triglycerides react with the alcohol to form fatty acid alkyl esters (biodiesel) and glycerol (a co-product).
    Triglyceride + Alcohol --(Catalyst)--> Biodiesel (Fatty Acid Esters) + Glycerol
  3. Separation: The denser glycerol settles at the bottom and is separated. The biodiesel layer is then purified (washed to remove catalyst, unreacted alcohol, and soaps) and dried.

Key Properties Relevant to CI Engines:

  • Cetane Number: Generally higher than or comparable to petroleum diesel, leading to shorter ignition delay and smoother combustion.
  • Energy Content: Slightly lower energy content (around 10-12% less by volume) than petroleum diesel, resulting in a slight reduction in power and fuel economy.
  • Lubricity: Excellent lubricity, which can reduce wear in fuel injection pumps and injectors, especially with modern low-sulfur diesel fuels that have reduced lubricity.
  • Viscosity: Higher viscosity than petroleum diesel, which can be an issue at low temperatures (poor flow, filter clogging).
  • Flash Point: Higher flash point, making it safer to handle and store.
  • Sulfur Content: Very low or negligible sulfur content, leading to reduced SOx emissions.
  • Oxygen Content: Biodiesel is an oxygenated fuel (contains about 10-11% oxygen by weight), which aids in more complete combustion, reducing particulate matter (PM), unburnt hydrocarbons (HC), and carbon monoxide (CO) emissions. However, it can sometimes lead to slightly higher NOx emissions.
  • Cold Flow Properties (Cloud Point, Pour Point): Generally poorer than petroleum diesel, meaning it can solidify or form wax crystals at lower temperatures, potentially causing fuel line or filter blockage. Additives or blending with petroleum diesel can improve this.
  • Biodegradability: More readily biodegradable than petroleum diesel, reducing environmental impact in case of spills.

Q4: Discuss the potential of Biogas as a fuel for IC engines. What are its main components and challenges?

Potential of Biogas:

Biogas offers significant potential as a renewable fuel for IC engines (both SI and modified CI engines). It is produced from the anaerobic digestion of organic matter (e.g., animal manure, agricultural waste, sewage sludge, food waste).

  • Renewable Source: Derived from organic waste, making it sustainable.
  • Waste Management: Its production helps in managing organic wastes effectively, reducing landfill burden and pollution.
  • Reduced GHG Emissions: Using biogas captures methane (a potent GHG) that would otherwise be released into the atmosphere from decomposing waste. While CO2 is produced on combustion, it is biogenic CO2 (part of the natural carbon cycle if feedstock is sustainable).
  • Decentralized Energy Production: Can be produced locally, especially in rural areas, providing energy independence.
  • Applications: Can be used in stationary engines for power generation, in vehicles (after upgrading/purification), or for heating/cooking.

Main Components of Biogas:

  • Methane (CH4): Typically 50-75%. This is the primary combustible component.
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO2): Typically 25-50%. This is an inert gas and dilutes the energy content.
  • Trace Gases: Small amounts of Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S), Nitrogen (N2), Oxygen (O2), water vapor, siloxanes, etc.

Challenges in Using Biogas in IC Engines:

  • Low Energy Density: Due to the high CO2 content and inherent properties of methane, raw biogas has a lower volumetric energy density than natural gas or conventional liquid fuels. This requires larger storage tanks or results in reduced range for vehicles.
  • Variable Composition: The composition of biogas can vary depending on the feedstock and digester conditions, affecting its calorific value and combustion characteristics.
  • Presence of Impurities:
    • Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S): Highly corrosive to engine components and forms SOx upon combustion. Needs to be removed (scrubbed).
    • Carbon Dioxide (CO2): Reduces the calorific value and can affect flame speed and stability. CO2 removal (upgrading biogas to biomethane) improves fuel quality but adds cost.
    • Water Vapor: Can cause corrosion and reduce heating value. Needs to be dried.
    • Siloxanes: Can form abrasive silica deposits in engines.
  • Low Flame Speed: Methane has a lower flame speed compared to gasoline, which can lead to incomplete combustion in unmodified SI engines. Engine modifications (e.g., advanced ignition timing, higher compression ratio, modified combustion chamber) are often needed.
  • Knocking Tendency: While methane has a high octane number, the overall knocking resistance can be influenced by other components and engine design.
  • Storage and Transportation: Storing gaseous fuel requires high-pressure tanks or liquefaction, adding complexity and cost.
  • Engine Modification: For optimal performance, SI engines may need adjustments to air-fuel ratio control, ignition system, and compression ratio. CI engines typically need to be run in dual-fuel mode (biogas + small amount of diesel pilot injection) or converted to spark-ignition.

Q5: What are the advantages and challenges of using Hydrogen as a fuel in IC engines?

Hydrogen (H2) is considered a promising long-term alternative fuel due to its clean combustion characteristics.

Advantages of Using Hydrogen in IC Engines:

  1. Zero Carbon Emissions at Point of Use: Combustion of hydrogen produces primarily water vapor (2H2 + O2 → 2H2O). No CO, CO2, or unburnt hydrocarbon (HC) emissions related to carbon.
  2. High Energy Content by Mass: Hydrogen has a very high energy density per unit mass (approx. 120 MJ/kg), about three times that of gasoline.
  3. Wide Flammability Limits: Hydrogen can burn in a wide range of air-fuel mixtures, allowing for very lean operation, which can improve thermal efficiency and reduce NOx emissions.
  4. High Auto-Ignition Temperature: This allows for higher compression ratios in SI engines, potentially increasing thermal efficiency.
  5. High Flame Speed: Results in combustion closer to the ideal constant volume process, potentially improving efficiency.
  6. Potential for Reduced NOx: Lean burn operation can significantly reduce NOx formation. However, at stoichiometric mixtures, NOx can be high due to high flame temperatures.

Challenges of Using Hydrogen in IC Engines:

  1. Production: Most hydrogen today is produced from fossil fuels (steam methane reforming - SMR), which releases CO2. "Green" hydrogen production (e.g., via electrolysis using renewable electricity) is currently expensive and less widespread.
  2. Storage:
    • Low Volumetric Energy Density: As a gas, hydrogen has very low energy density by volume, even when highly compressed. This requires large, heavy, high-pressure tanks (350-700 bar) for adequate vehicle range.
    • Liquid Hydrogen Storage: Requires cryogenic temperatures (-253°C), leading to complex, insulated tanks and boil-off losses.
    • Solid-State Storage: Materials like metal hydrides or sorbents can store hydrogen, but they often have issues with weight, cost, and refueling time.
  3. Material Compatibility: Hydrogen can cause embrittlement in some metals, requiring specialized materials for fuel systems and engine components.
  4. Combustion Issues:
    • Pre-ignition and Backfire: Due to low ignition energy and wide flammability, hydrogen mixtures can easily pre-ignite from hot spots or backfire into the intake manifold. Direct injection can mitigate this.
    • NOx Formation: While carbon emissions are zero, NOx can be a significant issue if combustion occurs at high temperatures (especially near stoichiometric mixtures). Lean burn or exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) is needed to control NOx.
  5. Infrastructure: Lack of a widespread hydrogen production, distribution, and refueling infrastructure is a major barrier.
  6. Cost: Both hydrogen fuel itself (especially green hydrogen) and hydrogen-compatible engine/vehicle technologies are currently more expensive than conventional options.
  7. Safety Concerns: Hydrogen is highly flammable and has a wider flammability range than gasoline. While it disperses quickly, robust safety systems and public perception are important considerations.

Q1: Draw and explain the P-V and T-S diagrams for an ideal Brayton cycle. Name the components of a simple gas turbine plant corresponding to these processes.

The ideal Brayton cycle is the theoretical cycle for gas turbines. It consists of four internally reversible processes:

  • 1-2: Isentropic Compression: Air is drawn into a compressor and compressed.
  • 2-3: Constant Pressure Heat Addition: Compressed air passes through a combustion chamber where fuel is burned, and heat is added at constant pressure.
  • 3-4: Isentropic Expansion: Hot gases expand through a turbine, producing work.
  • 4-1: Constant Pressure Heat Rejection: Exhaust gases are cooled down to the initial state (in a closed cycle) or expelled to the atmosphere (in an open cycle).

Components:

  • 1-2: Compressor
  • 2-3: Combustion Chamber (Combustor)
  • 3-4: Turbine
  • 4-1: Heat Exchanger (for cooling in closed cycle) / Atmosphere (for exhaust in open cycle)

  • 2-3: Horizontal line to the right (Constant Pressure Heat Addition)
  • 3-4: Curve downwards, V increases (Isentropic Expansion)

  • 1-2: Vertical line upwards (Isentropic Compression, S = constant)
  • 2-3: Curve upwards and to the right along a constant pressure line (Constant Pressure Heat Addition)
  • 3-4: Vertical line downwards (Isentropic Expansion, S = constant)
  • 4-1: Curve downwards and to the left along a constant pressure line (Constant Pressure Heat Rejection)

Q2: Explain the effects of Intercooling, Reheating, and Regeneration on the performance of a Brayton cycle.

These are methods to improve the performance (thermal efficiency and/or specific work output) of a Brayton cycle:

  • Intercooling:
    • Process: Compression is done in multiple stages, and the air is cooled (usually by a heat exchanger called an intercooler) between stages.
    • Effect on Cycle:
      • Reduces Compressor Work: Cooling the air reduces its specific volume, so less work is required for subsequent compression stages.
      • Increases Net Work Output: Since compressor work is reduced, the net work output (Turbine Work - Compressor Work) increases for the same turbine work.
      • Effect on Efficiency: By itself, intercooling might decrease thermal efficiency if the heat rejected in the intercooler is not utilized. However, it allows for a higher pressure ratio for the same compressor exit temperature or reduces the final compressor temperature for a given pressure ratio.
      • When Combined: Intercooling is most beneficial when combined with regeneration, as the lower compressor exit temperature (T2') increases the potential for heat recovery from the turbine exhaust in the regenerator.
  • Reheating:
    • Process: Expansion in the turbine is done in multiple stages. After the first stage of expansion, the gases are reheated (heat added again in a reheat combustor) before entering the next turbine stage.
    • Effect on Cycle:
      • Increases Turbine Work: Reheating increases the average temperature at which heat is added during expansion (partially) and increases the specific volume of gases entering subsequent turbine stages, thus increasing the total turbine work output.
      • Increases Net Work Output: As turbine work increases, net work output increases.
      • Effect on Efficiency: By itself, reheating generally decreases thermal efficiency because more heat is added, and the average temperature of heat addition might not increase proportionally.
      • When Combined: Reheating is beneficial for increasing specific power output. When combined with regeneration, the higher turbine exhaust temperature (T4') after reheating can enhance the effectiveness of regeneration.
  • Regeneration (Recuperation):
    • Process: A heat exchanger (regenerator or recuperator) is used to transfer heat from the hot turbine exhaust gases to the compressed air before it enters the combustion chamber.
    • Effect on Cycle:
      • Reduces External Heat Input: Since the compressed air is preheated, less fuel is needed in the combustion chamber to reach the desired turbine inlet temperature.
      • Increases Thermal Efficiency: By reducing the fuel consumption (heat input) for the same net work output, thermal efficiency is significantly increased.
      • Effect on Net Work Output: Regeneration itself does not directly affect the net work output (turbine work and compressor work remain the same for ideal components).
      • Condition for Benefit: For regeneration to be effective, the turbine exhaust temperature (T4) must be higher than the compressor discharge temperature (T2). This is often the case for lower pressure ratios.

Overall:

  • Intercooling and Reheating primarily increase the net work output.
  • Regeneration primarily increases the thermal efficiency.
  • A combination of all three (Intercooling + Reheating + Regeneration) can lead to both high specific work output and high thermal efficiency.

Q3: What is cogeneration? Explain its principle and benefits with a simple example.

Cogeneration (Combined Heat and Power - CHP):

Cogeneration is the simultaneous production of two or more forms of useful energy from a single primary energy source. Typically, it involves the production of electricity (or mechanical power) and useful thermal energy (heat, e.g., steam, hot water) from the same fuel input.

Principle:

Conventional power plants (e.g., large thermal power stations) convert only a portion of the fuel's energy into electricity (typically 30-50%), and the remaining majority is rejected to the environment as waste heat (e.g., through cooling towers or exhaust gases).
Cogeneration systems are designed to capture and utilize a significant portion of this waste heat for industrial processes, space heating, water heating, or cooling (via absorption chillers). By making use of this otherwise wasted heat, the overall energy efficiency of the system is dramatically increased.

Benefits of Cogeneration:

  1. Increased Energy Efficiency: Overall fuel utilization efficiency can reach 80-90%, compared to 30-50% for conventional electricity-only generation plus the efficiency of a separate boiler for heat.
  2. Reduced Fuel Consumption: Since more useful energy is extracted from the same amount of fuel, less fuel is needed overall, leading to cost savings.
  3. Lower Emissions: Burning less fuel means lower emissions of greenhouse gases (like CO2) and other pollutants (NOx, SOx, PM) per unit of useful energy output.
  4. Reduced Energy Costs: Lower fuel consumption and potentially avoiding the need for separate heating systems lead to significant operational cost savings.
  5. Improved Energy Reliability and Security: On-site cogeneration can provide a more reliable power supply, especially if independent of the grid, and reduce reliance on centralized energy sources.
  6. Reduced Transmission and Distribution Losses: If power is generated close to the point of use, losses associated with long-distance electricity transmission are minimized.

Simple Example:

Consider an industrial facility (e.g., a paper mill, chemical plant, or a large hospital) that requires both electricity for its machinery and lighting, and steam for its processes or heating.

  • Separate Production (Conventional): The facility buys electricity from the grid (generated at ~40% efficiency) and operates its own boilers (at ~80% efficiency) to produce steam. A lot of waste heat is lost at the power plant and some from the boiler.
  • Cogeneration System: The facility installs a gas turbine or an IC engine coupled to a generator.
    1. Fuel (e.g., natural gas) is burned in the gas turbine/engine to produce electricity.
    2. The hot exhaust gases from the turbine/engine, instead of being vented to the atmosphere, are passed through a Heat Recovery Steam Generator (HRSG) or a heat exchanger.
    3. The HRSG uses the heat from the exhaust gases to produce the steam required by the facility.

In this cogeneration setup, the fuel is used to produce both electricity and steam, with much less waste heat. The overall efficiency might be 85%, significantly higher than the combined efficiency of separate production.

Q4: Classify gas turbines based on (a) cycle of operation and (b) arrangement of shafts.

Gas turbines can be classified based on several criteria:

(a) Based on Cycle of Operation:

  1. Open Cycle Gas Turbine:
    • The working fluid (air) is drawn from the atmosphere, compressed, heated in a combustion chamber (where fuel is burned directly in the air), expanded through the turbine, and then the exhaust gases are discharged back into the atmosphere.
    • The same air does not recirculate.
    • Most common type due to simplicity and direct use of combustion products.
    • Internal combustion occurs.
  2. Closed Cycle Gas Turbine:
    • The working fluid (e.g., air, helium, or other suitable gas) circulates in a closed loop.
    • After expansion in the turbine, the working fluid is cooled in a heat exchanger (cooler) before returning to the compressor inlet.
    • Heat is added to the working fluid from an external source via another heat exchanger (heater) instead of direct fuel combustion in the working fluid. This means the fuel and its combustion products do not mix with the working fluid.
    • Allows use of various working fluids and fuels (including those unsuitable for open cycles, like coal or nuclear). Generally more complex and expensive.

(b) Based on Arrangement of Shafts:

  1. Single Shaft Gas Turbine:
    • The compressor, turbine, and the driven load (e.g., an electric generator) are all mounted on a single, common shaft.
    • The entire unit operates at the same speed (or a speed dictated by a gearbox if present for the load).
    • Simpler construction.
    • Suitable for applications requiring constant speed operation, like power generation. Starting can be more challenging as the entire rotating mass needs to be brought up to speed.
  2. Multi-Shaft Gas Turbine (Commonly Two-Shaft):
    • The turbine is split into two or more sections, each on a separate shaft.
    • Two-Shaft Arrangement (Typical):
      • Gas Generator Turbine (High-Pressure Turbine): This turbine is directly connected to and drives the compressor. This combination is called the "gas generator."
      • Power Turbine (Low-Pressure Turbine or Free Turbine): This turbine is on a separate shaft, mechanically independent of the gas generator shaft. It extracts the remaining energy from the hot gases exiting the gas generator turbine and drives the external load (e.g., propeller, pump, or generator).
    • Advantages:
      • The power turbine can operate at a wide range of speeds, independent of the gas generator speed. This is very useful for applications with variable speed requirements, such as vehicle propulsion, marine propulsion, or driving pumps/compressors.
      • Better part-load performance and starting characteristics.
      • Allows the gas generator to operate closer to its optimal design speed.

Q5: What are the main applications of gas turbines?

Gas turbines are versatile power-generating units with a wide range of applications due to their high power-to-weight ratio, reliability, and quick start-up capabilities.

  1. Aircraft Propulsion:
    • Turbojets: For high-speed aircraft (military jets, some older commercial jets).
    • Turbofans: Dominant engine type for modern commercial and military transport aircraft due to better fuel efficiency at subsonic speeds.
    • Turboprops: For lower-speed, shorter-haul aircraft, offering good efficiency at these speeds.
    • Turboshafts: For helicopters and as auxiliary power units (APUs) on aircraft.
  2. Power Generation:
    • Peaking Power Plants: Gas turbines can be started and stopped quickly, making them ideal for meeting peak electricity demands.
    • Base Load Power Plants (Combined Cycle): In combined cycle gas turbine (CCGT) plants, the hot exhaust from the gas turbine is used to generate steam, which then drives a steam turbine, significantly increasing overall plant efficiency (often exceeding 60%). These are used for base-load power.
    • Standby and Emergency Power: For hospitals, data centers, and critical facilities.
    • Distributed Generation: Smaller gas turbines for on-site power generation for industrial facilities or communities.
  3. Oil and Gas Industry:
    • Driving Compressors: For natural gas pipelines and processing plants.
    • Driving Pumps: For oil pipelines and offshore platforms.
    • Offshore Power Generation: Providing electricity for offshore oil and gas platforms.
  4. Marine Propulsion:
    • Naval Vessels: Warships often use gas turbines for high-speed propulsion due to their high power density (e.g., COGAG - Combined Gas and Gas, CODAG - Combined Diesel and Gas arrangements).
    • Fast Ferries and Luxury Yachts: For high-speed commercial and leisure craft.
  5. Industrial Applications:
    • Mechanical Drive: Driving pumps, compressors, and other large rotating equipment in industries like chemical processing, refining, and manufacturing.
    • Cogeneration (Combined Heat and Power - CHP): Providing both electricity and useful heat (steam or hot water) for industrial processes or district heating, significantly improving overall energy efficiency.
  6. Land Transportation (Less Common but notable):
    • Experimental Cars and Trucks: Some experimental vehicles have used gas turbines.
    • Racing Cars: E.g., the Lotus 56 Indy car.
    • Heavy Vehicles/Tanks: Some military tanks (e.g., M1 Abrams) use gas turbines for their high power-to-weight ratio.

Q1: Explain the working principle of a simple turbojet engine with a neat sketch.

A turbojet engine is a type of gas turbine engine optimized to produce thrust for aircraft propulsion by accelerating a relatively small mass of air to a very high velocity.

Working Principle (based on Brayton cycle):

  1. Intake/Diffuser: Air from the atmosphere enters the engine through an intake (diffuser). The diffuser slows down the incoming air and increases its static pressure (ram effect at high speeds).
  2. Compressor: The air is then drawn into a multi-stage axial or centrifugal compressor, where its pressure and temperature are significantly increased. The compressor is driven by the turbine.
  3. Combustion Chamber (Combustor): The high-pressure, high-temperature air from the compressor enters the combustion chamber. Fuel is injected and burned continuously in this air, further increasing the temperature and energy of the gases at nearly constant pressure.
  4. Turbine: The hot, high-energy gases from the combustor expand through a turbine. The turbine extracts just enough energy from these gases to drive the compressor (and any auxiliaries).
  5. Nozzle (Propelling Nozzle): The gases exiting the turbine still possess high temperature and pressure. These gases are then accelerated to a very high velocity (supersonic in many cases) as they expand through a convergent-divergent or convergent nozzle at the rear of the engine.
  6. Thrust Production: According to Newton's third law, the action of expelling these high-velocity gases rearwards produces an equal and opposite reaction force forwards, which is the thrust that propels the aircraft.
    Thrust (F) ≈ ṁe * Vj - ṁa * Va (where ṁe is exhaust mass flow, Vj is jet velocity, ṁa is inlet air mass flow, Va is aircraft velocity; neglecting pressure thrust for simplicity here).

Q3: What is thrust augmentation? Briefly explain two common methods.

Thrust augmentation refers to methods used to temporarily increase the thrust output of a jet engine, typically for short durations when maximum thrust is required, such as during take-off, combat maneuvers, or climbing.

Two common methods are:

  1. Afterburning (Reheat):
    • Principle: Fuel is injected and burned in the hot, oxygen-rich exhaust gases downstream of the turbine but before the propelling nozzle.
    • Process: The exhaust duct between the turbine and the nozzle is essentially converted into a secondary combustion chamber. Since the turbine has already extracted work, the temperature of gases in the afterburner can be raised to very high levels (much higher than turbine inlet temperature limits).
    • Effect: This additional combustion significantly increases the temperature and specific volume of the exhaust gases, leading to a much higher jet velocity and thus a substantial increase in thrust (can be 50% or more).
    • Disadvantages:
      • Extremely high fuel consumption when active.
      • Adds weight and complexity to the engine.
      • Increases engine noise and infrared signature.
    • Applications: Primarily used in military combat aircraft for supersonic flight and enhanced maneuverability, and some supersonic commercial aircraft (e.g., Concorde).
  2. Water Injection (or Water-Methanol Injection):
    • Principle: Water (or a water-methanol mixture) is injected into the engine, typically at the compressor inlet or diffuser, or sometimes directly into the combustion chamber.
    • Process & Effect:
      • At Compressor Inlet/Diffuser: The evaporation of water cools the incoming air. Cooler air is denser, so the compressor can handle a greater mass flow rate of air. This increased mass flow through the engine results in higher thrust. The cooling effect also allows the engine to run at a higher power setting without exceeding temperature limits.
      • In Combustion Chamber: Injection into the combustor can increase the mass flow rate through the turbine and nozzle. The water turns into steam, adding to the mass of gases being expelled. Methanol, if used, also acts as an additional fuel.
    • Advantages:
      • Simpler and lighter than afterburners for a moderate thrust increase.
      • Can improve take-off performance, especially in hot weather or from high-altitude airfields ("hot and high" conditions).
    • Disadvantages:
      • Limited duration of use (depends on the amount of water carried).
      • Requires a tank and system for water storage and injection.
      • Can cause thermal shock or corrosion if not designed properly.
    • Applications: Used in some older jet engines and some turboprops to boost take-off thrust.

Q4: Describe the desirable characteristics of a gas turbine combustion chamber.

The combustion chamber (combustor or burner) is a critical component of a gas turbine engine. Its primary function is to efficiently burn large quantities of fuel with the compressed air, adding thermal energy to the flow. Desirable characteristics include:

  1. High Combustion Efficiency: It should burn the fuel as completely as possible over a wide range of operating conditions (from idle to full power, and at various altitudes). This minimizes fuel wastage and reduces emissions of unburnt hydrocarbons and carbon monoxide. Typically >99%.
  2. Low Pressure Loss: The pressure drop across the combustor should be minimal. High pressure loss reduces the overall cycle efficiency and engine performance.
  3. Stable Operation: Combustion must be stable and continuous, without flameout (extinction of flame) or pulsations, across the entire engine operating envelope.
  4. Wide Operating Range: Must light up easily and operate reliably from sea level to high altitudes, and from low to high air mass flow rates and temperatures.
  5. Uniform Turbine Inlet Temperature Profile: The temperature distribution of the gases entering the turbine should be as uniform as possible (both radially and circumferentially) to avoid hot spots on turbine blades, which can lead to damage and reduced life. Some profiling (hotter at mid-span) is often designed.
  6. Low Emissions: It should produce minimal harmful emissions, such as oxides of nitrogen (NOx), carbon monoxide (CO), unburnt hydrocarbons (UHC), and soot (particulate matter).
  7. Durability and Long Life: The combustor liner and components must withstand extremely high temperatures and thermal stresses for extended periods. Effective cooling of the liner is crucial.
  8. Compact Size and Low Weight: Especially important for aircraft engines, the combustor should be as small and light as possible for a given heat release rate.
  9. Easy Starting/Relight Capability: The combustor must allow for reliable ignition on the ground and relight capability in the air if a flameout occurs.
  10. Cost-Effectiveness: Should be manufacturable at a reasonable cost.
  11. No Carbon Deposition: Should resist the formation of carbon deposits (coking) on the liner walls or fuel nozzles, which can affect performance and durability.

Q5: What are the main sources of emissions from gas turbines, and what pollutants are of primary concern?

Gas turbine emissions originate from the combustion of hydrocarbon fuels (like kerosene, natural gas, or diesel) with air. The main sources are the combustion process itself and, to a lesser extent, fuel impurities.

Pollutants of Primary Concern:

  1. Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx):
    • Source: Formed primarily by the reaction of atmospheric nitrogen (N2) and oxygen (O2) at high temperatures in the combustion chamber. The main forms are nitric oxide (NO) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2), collectively referred to as NOx.
      • Thermal NOx: Dominant mechanism, formation rate increases exponentially with temperature.
      • Prompt NOx: Forms in fuel-rich zones early in the flame.
      • Fuel NOx: Forms from nitrogen compounds present in the fuel (less significant for clean fuels like natural gas or jet fuel).
    • Impact: Contribute to smog formation, acid rain, respiratory problems, and ozone depletion.
  2. Carbon Monoxide (CO):
    • Source: Results from incomplete combustion of the hydrocarbon fuel, where there is insufficient oxygen or residence time at high temperature to convert all carbon to CO2.
    • Impact: Toxic gas that impairs oxygen delivery in the blood. Also a precursor to ground-level ozone. CO emissions are typically higher at low power settings (idle) when combustion temperatures are lower.
  3. Unburnt Hydrocarbons (UHC or HC):
    • Source: Consist of fuel molecules that are not burned or only partially burned during combustion. Caused by poor atomization, insufficient mixing, flame quenching near cool walls, or very lean mixtures.
    • Impact: Contribute to smog formation, some HCs are carcinogenic, and they represent wasted fuel energy. Higher at low power settings.
  4. Particulate Matter (PM) / Soot:
    • Source: Fine solid particles (primarily carbon) formed in fuel-rich, high-temperature zones within the combustor. Visible as smoke.
    • Impact: Respiratory problems, reduced visibility, contribute to global warming (black carbon), and can deposit on engine components.
  5. Sulfur Oxides (SOx):
    • Source: Formed from sulfur compounds present in the fuel (e.g., sulfur in diesel or some heavier oils). Jet fuels and natural gas have very low sulfur content, so SOx is less of an issue for them.
    • Impact: Contribute to acid rain and respiratory problems.
  6. Carbon Dioxide (CO2):
    • Source: An inherent product of complete combustion of any hydrocarbon fuel.
    • Impact: The primary greenhouse gas contributing to global warming and climate change. While not a "pollutant" in the traditional sense of direct toxicity, its environmental impact is a major concern.
  7. Water Vapor (H2O):
    • Source: An inherent product of complete combustion of hydrocarbon fuel.
    • Impact: A greenhouse gas, but its contribution from aviation at cruise altitudes (forming contrails) has a more complex effect on climate.

Efforts to reduce these emissions involve advanced combustor designs (e.g., lean-burn, rich-quench-lean), catalytic converters (for stationary turbines), and use of cleaner fuels.

The isentropic efficiencies of the compressor and turbine are 88% and 92% respectively. The nozzle efficiency is 95%. Assume the exhaust gases expand in the nozzle to the ambient pressure of 0.9 bar. Neglect the mass of fuel. Calculate: a) The pressure and temperature at the nozzle exit. b) The exit jet velocity. c) The specific thrust. Take γ = 1.4 and Cp = 1.005 kJ/kgK for air and gas. R = 0.287 kJ/kgK.

Given: rp_overall = P2/P1 = 12, P1 = 0.9 bar, T1 = 280 K, T3 (Turbine Inlet Temp) = 1400 K.
ηc = 0.88, ηt = 0.92, ηn (nozzle efficiency) = 0.95.

    P2 = rp_overall * P1 = 12 * 0.9 = 10.8 bar
